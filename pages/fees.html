Understanding what fees are legal and what you should refuse to pay

Tenant Fees Act 2019

Most letting agent fees are now banned under the Tenant Fees Act 2019 . This important legislation protects tenants from excessive and hidden charges that were previously charged by letting agents.

The ONLY Permitted Payments

Letting agents can only legally charge tenants for:

  • Rent - Your monthly or periodic rent payments
  • Refundable Deposit - Protected in an approved scheme (typically 5 weeks' rent maximum)
  • Holding Deposit - Small refundable deposit to reserve a property (maximum 5% of first month's rent)

That's it. Nothing else.

Commonly BANNED Fees

You should NEVER pay these charges - they are illegal:

  • ❌ Application fees
  • ❌ Administration fees
  • ❌ Referencing fees (credit checks)
  • ❌ Guarantor fees
  • ❌ Early termination fees
  • ❌ Renewal fees
  • ❌ Inventory fees
  • ❌ Check-in/check-out inspection fees
  • ❌ Pet fees (though landlord can refuse pets)
  • ❌ Cleaning fees (cannot be charged upfront)
  • ❌ Tenancy agreement fees
  • ❌ Key replacement fees (except actual cost if you lost them)

What If You're Asked to Pay Illegal Fees?

  1. Refuse to pay - You have no legal obligation
  2. Request refund in writing - If you already paid
  3. Report to local council - Contact your local council enforcement team
  4. Report to Citizens Advice - They can guide you further
  5. Contact Shelter - Free advice on your rights
  6. Claim through court - You can recover illegal fees through the County Court

Red Flags When Viewing Properties

Be cautious of letting agents who:

  • Ask for upfront "application fees" or "administration fees"
  • Charge for credit checks or referencing
  • Require payment before you've signed the tenancy agreement
  • Won't provide itemized fees in writing
  • Pressure you to pay quickly
  • Ask for cash payments (no paper trail)

Key Case Law

The Tenant Fees Act 2019 came into force on 1 June 2019. If you paid illegal fees before this date, you may still be able to claim them back within six years. Seek legal advice if you believe this applies to you.

Holding Deposits Explained

A holding deposit is the only fee (other than rent and deposit) that is legally allowed. However, it must follow strict rules:

  • Maximum: 5% of the first month's rent
  • It should be refundable
  • It becomes part of your deposit if you go ahead
  • It should be returned if you withdraw your application or the landlord rejects you
  • It should be refunded if the landlord changes their mind
