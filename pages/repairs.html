Repairs & maintenance.

Your landlord has a legal duty to keep your home in good repair. Here is what they must fix, and what to do when they don't.

📋

What Your Landlord Must Maintain

Statutory obligations under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, s.11

Section 11 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 requires landlords to keep the following in repair and proper working order throughout the tenancy. This cannot be contracted out of — any clause in your tenancy agreement that tries to pass these responsibilities to you is unenforceable.

🔥
Heating & hot waterBoiler and central heating system must be kept in working order.
⚡
Electrical installationsWiring, sockets, fuse boards, and fixed electrical fittings must be safe and functional.
🚰
Water & plumbingPipes, taps, drains, and the supply of cold and hot water must be maintained.
🏠
Structure & exteriorRoof, walls, foundations, floors, ceilings, and external paintwork must be kept in repair.
🔒
Windows & doorsMust close and lock properly, and be weatherproof.
🔥
Gas installationsGas pipes, appliances provided by the landlord, and flues must be kept safe. Annual gas safety checks are required by law.
🚽
SanitationToilet, sink, bath or shower, and drainage systems must be in working order.
💧
Damp & mouldWhere caused by a structural defect or the landlord's failure to repair, this is the landlord's responsibility under the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018.
Legal basis: Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, s.11 and the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018.
📝

How to Report a Repair

Following these steps protects you if the matter escalates into a formal dispute.

1

Report in writing. Email is best — it creates an automatic timestamp. Describe the problem clearly: what it is, where it is, and when you first noticed it.

2

Photograph the problem. Take dated photos or a short video. Store them somewhere safe — you may need them later as evidence.

3

Give reasonable time. For non-urgent repairs, 14 days is generally considered reasonable. For emergencies (no heating in winter, gas leak, flooding), the landlord should respond within hours or the same day.

4

Chase in writing if ignored. Send a follow-up email referring back to your original report. Keep a copy of everything.

5

Send a formal letter. If still no action, use the Letter Generator to produce a letter citing Section 11 and setting a clear deadline.

6

Contact your local council. If your landlord still does not act, report the matter to your council's Environmental Health team. They can inspect the property and, if necessary, issue a formal improvement notice to your landlord.

🚨

Emergency Repairs

Important: Do not deduct repair costs from your rent without first getting legal advice. Doing so without following the correct process could put you at risk of rent arrears proceedings.

Emergencies — such as a broken boiler in winter, a burst pipe, a gas leak, or total loss of electricity — require your landlord to act quickly. What to do:

1

Contact your landlord or agent immediately, and follow up in writing by email the same day.

2

If there is a risk to safety (gas leak, electrical hazard, flooding), contact National Gas Emergency (0800 111 999) or your utility provider directly.

3

If the landlord is unreachable and the repair is genuinely urgent, you may in some cases arrange and pay for emergency repairs yourself — but you should seek advice from Shelter (0808 800 4444) or Citizens Advice first before doing this.

4

Keep all receipts and written records. If your landlord refuses to reimburse you, you may be able to recover costs, but this will need legal advice specific to your situation.

🏡

Normal Wear and Tear vs. Damage

Your landlord is responsible for repairs and maintenance. You are responsible for damage you have caused. Normal wear and tear — the gradual deterioration that happens through ordinary everyday use — is never your financial responsibility.

Landlord's responsibility

  • Carpets worn through normal use over time
  • Paint that has faded or scuffed with everyday use
  • Appliances that have worn out through age
  • Loose hinges or stiff door handles
  • Structural cracks or damp from poor construction

Tenant's responsibility

  • Stains, burns, or tears you caused
  • Holes or damage from picture hooks (beyond minor)
  • Items you broke through misuse or carelessness
  • Neglecting to report a problem, causing it to worsen
  • Pet damage
