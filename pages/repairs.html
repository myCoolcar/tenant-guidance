How to report a repair

Follow these steps in order — they protect you if the dispute escalates

1

Report in writing

Email is best — it creates a clear record. Describe the problem: what it is, where it is, and when you first noticed it.

Why writing matters: If your landlord later claims they were never told, a timestamped email is your evidence. Keep the email polite and factual — avoid emotive language. If you have already reported verbally, follow up immediately by email saying "As discussed on [date], I am reporting the following issue…"
2

Document the problem

Take dated photos or a short video of the issue. Store copies somewhere safe — cloud storage, email to yourself, or both. This is your evidence.

Tip: Most phones embed the date and time in photo. Take photos clearly showing the extent of the problem. For damp or mould, photograph affected areas widely and closely. If the issue worsens over time, photograph it regularly to demonstrate your landlord's inaction.
3

Allow reasonable time

For standard repairs, 14 days is generally reasonable. For urgent issues (no heating in winter, flooding), the landlord should respond the same day or within hours. Log the date you first reported the issue using the Timeline Tool.

What counts as reasonable? Courts consider the nature and severity of the repair when deciding what is "reasonable". A dripping tap might warrant 28 days; a broken boiler in January warrants same-day action. There is no fixed rule in law — but 14 days for non-urgent repairs is widely accepted as a guideline.
4

Chase in writing

If you have heard nothing after the deadline, send a follow-up email referencing your original report and giving a further deadline. Keep every copy.

Example wording: "I am writing to follow up on my email of [date] regarding [problem]. You have not responded or arranged a repair. I am requesting that this is resolved within [7 days]. Please confirm receipt of this email."
5

Send a formal repair letter

If still no action, send a formal letter citing Section 11 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 and setting a clear final deadline. Use the Letter Generator to produce one in under a minute.

6

Contact your local council

Report the issue to your council's Environmental Health team. They can inspect your property and issue a formal improvement notice or take enforcement action against your landlord.

Environmental Health officers use the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) to assess hazards. A category 1 hazard (most serious) requires the council to take action. This service is free and the council cannot tell your landlord who reported unless necessary. Find your local council at gov.uk/find-local-council.
7

Consider legal action

As a last resort, you may be able to apply to the county court for an order requiring your landlord to carry out repairs, and claim compensation. Get advice from Shelter (0808 800 4444) or Citizens Advice before taking this step.

Emergency repairs

When your landlord must act immediately — same day or within hours

⚠️ Do not deduct repair costs from your rent without first getting legal advice. Doing so without following the correct process could put you at risk of rent arrears proceedings, even if your landlord is clearly at fault.

The following situations are emergencies. Your landlord must respond urgently — failure to do so may expose them to enforcement action and compensation claims.

🔥
Broken boiler in winter No heating or hot water in cold weather is a serious hazard, especially for children, elderly, or disabled occupants.
🌊
Flooding or burst pipe Water ingress can cause structural damage and create electrical hazards. Requires immediate action.
⚡
Electrical hazard Exposed wiring, sparking sockets, or total loss of electricity pose an immediate risk to life.
⚠️
Gas leak or carbon monoxide Leave the property immediately. Do not use any switches. Call the National Gas Emergency line.
1
Contact your landlord or agent immediately by phone, then follow up in writing by email the same day to create a record.
2
For a gas leak, leave the property, do not operate any switches, and call the National Gas Emergency Service on 0800 111 999 (free, 24/7). For a carbon monoxide alarm, evacuate and call 999.
3
If the landlord is genuinely unreachable and the repair is a risk to health or safety, you may be able to arrange emergency repairs yourself — but speak to Shelter (0808 800 4444) or Citizens Advice first. Keep all receipts.
4
If your landlord refuses to reimburse reasonable emergency costs, you may be able to recover them, but this requires legal advice specific to your situation. See the Resources page for free support.

What your landlord must maintain

Statutory obligations under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, s.11 — these cannot be signed away

Section 11 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 requires landlords to keep the following in repair and proper working order throughout the tenancy. Any clause in your tenancy agreement that tries to pass these responsibilities to you is unenforceable.

🔥
Heating & hot waterBoiler and central heating system must be kept in working order.
⚡
Electrical installationsWiring, sockets, fuse boards, and fixed electrical fittings must be safe and functional.
🚰
Water & plumbingPipes, taps, drains, and the supply of cold and hot water must be maintained.
🏠
Structure & exteriorRoof, walls, foundations, floors, ceilings, and external paintwork must be kept in repair.
🔒
Windows & doorsMust close and lock properly, and be weatherproof.
🔥
Gas installationsGas pipes, appliances provided by the landlord, and flues must be safe. Annual gas safety checks are required by law.
🚽
SanitationToilet, sink, bath or shower, and drainage systems must be in working order.
💧
Damp & mouldWhere caused by a structural defect or the landlord's failure to repair — the landlord's responsibility under the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018.
Legal basis: Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, s.11 and the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018.

Normal wear and tear vs. damage

The gradual deterioration from everyday use is never your financial responsibility

Your landlord is responsible for maintenance and repair. You are responsible for damage you have caused. Normal wear and tear — the expected decline through ordinary use — is not your liability, even at the end of the tenancy.

✅ Landlord's responsibility

  • Carpets worn through normal use over time
  • Paint that has faded or scuffed with everyday use
  • Appliances that have worn out through age
  • Loose hinges or stiff door handles
  • Structural cracks or damp from poor construction

⚠️ Tenant's responsibility

  • Stains, burns, or tears you caused
  • Holes or damage from picture hooks (beyond minor)
  • Items broken through misuse or carelessness
  • Neglecting to report a problem, causing it to worsen
  • Pet damage
