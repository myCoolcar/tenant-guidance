Landlord's Legal Obligations

Landlords have a legal duty to maintain the structure and installations of the property in a safe and functional condition. These obligations are defined in the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 Section 11 .

What Must Be Maintained:

  • Heating and boilers - Functional heating system and hot water supply
  • Electrical wiring and safety - Safe electrical installations
  • Water and plumbing - Working water supply and drainage
  • Structural repairs - Roof, walls, and structural integrity
  • Roof and external walls - Protection from weather and water damage
  • Windows and doors - Functional locks and weatherproofing
  • Gas installations and appliances - Safe gas appliances and installations
  • Sanitation - Functional toilet, sink, and waste systems

What You Should Do if Repairs Are Needed

  1. Report in writing - Email or send a letter describing the issue. This creates a record.
  2. Keep copies - Save all communications and correspondence
  3. Document the problem - Take photos or videos showing the issue
  4. Be specific - Include dates, times, and how the problem affects you
  5. Give reasonable time - Typically 14-30 days for non-emergency repairs
  6. Follow up - If no response, send a reminder in writing
  7. Seek help - Contact your local council if the landlord fails to respond

Emergency Repairs

For emergency repairs (no heating in winter, burst pipes, electrical hazards, loss of water supply), you may be able to take emergency action if your landlord is unresponsive.

  • Contact your landlord and give them limited time to respond
  • If unresponsive, you may arrange repairs yourself
  • Document everything and keep receipts
  • You may be able to deduct emergency repair costs from rent
  • Always seek advice before deducting from rent - Contact Citizens Advice or Shelter

If Your Landlord Refuses to Repair

  • Contact your local council's housing enforcement team
  • File a complaint with environmental health
  • Seek advice from Citizens Advice or Shelter
  • Consider joining a tenants' union for support
  • You may be able to pursue compensation through the courts

Normal Wear and Tear vs. Damage

Your landlord is responsible for repairs. You are responsible for normal wear and tear of items you have damaged.

Landlord's Responsibility:

  • Replacing worn-out carpet
  • Repainting faded walls
  • Fixing faulty plumbing or heating
  • Structural repairs

Your Responsibility:

  • Damage you caused (e.g., holes in walls from your picture frames)
  • Broken items you broke (e.g., windows you broke)
  • Cleaning (though landlord can't charge for normal wear and tear)
  • Maintenance you were specifically responsible for per the tenancy agreement
