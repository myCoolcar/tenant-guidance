Primary Legislation

Acts of Parliament that define tenant rights in England and Wales

Housing Act 1988
Act legislation.gov.uk ↗

Established the modern private rental framework in England, introducing assured and assured shorthold tenancies (ASTs). Sets out the legal procedures for possession — including Section 8 (grounds-based) and Section 21 (no-fault) notices — as well as rules on rent increases and tenancy terms. Most private tenants in England today hold an AST under this Act.

Landlord and Tenant Act 1985
Act legislation.gov.uk ↗

Section 11 places a statutory obligation on landlords to keep in repair the structure and exterior of a dwelling and its installations for the supply of water, gas, electricity, and sanitation, and for space and water heating. Applies to most residential lettings granted for a term of less than seven years and cannot be contracted out of.

Tenant Fees Act 2019
Act legislation.gov.uk ↗

Prohibits landlords and letting agents from requiring tenants to make payments not permitted by Schedule 1 of the Act. Banned charges include admin fees, referencing fees, check-in and check-out fees, and contract renewal fees. A first breach attracts a civil penalty of up to £5,000; a further breach within five years may result in a fine of up to £30,000 or a banning order.

Housing Act 2004
Act legislation.gov.uk ↗

Introduced mandatory tenancy deposit protection (ss.212–215), requiring deposits to be held in a government-approved scheme within 30 days of receipt and for prescribed information to be served on the tenant. Failure attracts a penalty of 1–3× the deposit amount. Also introduced the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS), enabling local authorities to take enforcement action against hazardous properties.

Protection from Eviction Act 1977
Act legislation.gov.uk ↗

Section 1 makes it a criminal offence for any person to unlawfully deprive a residential occupier of their occupation of premises, or to harass a residential occupier with intent to cause them to give up occupation or to refrain from exercising their rights. Covers acts such as changing locks, removing belongings, or cutting off utilities without a court order.

Deregulation Act 2015
Act legislation.gov.uk ↗

Introduced protections against retaliatory (revenge) eviction. Where a tenant makes a written complaint about the condition of a property and the landlord fails to respond adequately or serves a Section 21 notice within six months of the complaint, the notice may be invalid. Also introduced requirements for landlords to provide tenants with a gas safety certificate, EPC, and the How to Rent guide before serving a valid Section 21 notice.

Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) Regulations 2020
Regulations legislation.gov.uk ↗

Requires landlords of privately rented residential accommodation in England to have electrical installations inspected and tested by a qualified and competent person at least every five years. Landlords must provide a copy of the most recent Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR) to existing tenants within 28 days of the inspection, and to new tenants before they occupy the premises.

Key Case Law

Landmark judgments that shaped tenant rights in England and Wales

Street v Mountford [1985] AC 809 — House of Lords
Case Law BAILII ↗

Established the legal definition of a tenancy as distinct from a licence. The House of Lords held that where a person has exclusive possession of residential premises for a term at a rent, the arrangement constitutes a tenancy regardless of how the parties or the written agreement describe it. Landlords cannot use licence agreements to deprive occupiers of tenant protections.

Spencer v Taylor [2013] EWCA Civ 1600 — Court of Appeal
Case Law BAILII ↗

Clarified that a Section 21 notice served during a fixed term of an assured shorthold tenancy can be used to recover possession once the tenancy becomes periodic at the end of that fixed term, provided the notice period has not expired before the tenancy becomes periodic. Significantly affected how landlords serve possession notices under the Housing Act 1988.

Government-Approved Deposit Protection Schemes

Your landlord must use one of these three schemes

Under the Housing Act 2004, all deposits taken for assured shorthold tenancies in England must be protected in one of the following three government-authorised schemes within 30 days. Each scheme also provides a free dispute resolution service if deductions are contested.

Deposit Protection Service (DPS)

A custodial scheme — the DPS holds the deposit for the duration of the tenancy. Free to use. Provides adjudication for disputed deductions.

depositprotection.com ↗

MyDeposits

An insurance-based scheme — the landlord or agent holds the deposit and pays a fee to insure it. Dispute resolution service available to tenants at no cost.

mydeposits.co.uk ↗

Tenancy Deposit Scheme (TDS)

Offers both custodial and insurance-backed protection. TDS adjudication is available for tenants disputing deductions at the end of a tenancy.

tenancydepositscheme.com ↗

Housing Standards & Official Guidance

Government guidance and enforcement frameworks

Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) — Guidance for Landlords
Guidance gov.uk ↗

The HHSRS is the system used by local authority environmental health officers to assess hazards in residential properties. It covers 29 categories of hazard including damp and mould, excess cold, fire, falls, and electrical hazards. Councils can issue improvement notices, prohibition orders, or carry out works in default where serious hazards are identified. Tenants can request an inspection from their local council.

How to Rent: The Checklist for Renting in England — MHCLG
Guidance gov.uk ↗

The official government guide that landlords are legally required to provide to tenants at the start of an assured shorthold tenancy. Covers tenant and landlord rights and responsibilities, what to check before renting, and what to do if things go wrong. Failure to provide the current version can prevent a landlord from serving a valid Section 21 notice.

Organisations That Can Help

Free and specialist support for tenants

Shelter England
Organisation shelter.org.uk ↗

Shelter is the leading housing and homelessness charity in England. It provides free housing advice — online, over the phone (0808 800 4444), and via local advice centres — on all aspects of renting, including repairs, deposits, eviction, and homelessness. Shelter's website also contains detailed legal guidance written by housing law specialists.

Citizens Advice
Organisation citizensadvice.org.uk ↗

Citizens Advice provides free, confidential, and impartial advice on housing, benefits, debt, and legal issues. Advice is available online, by phone, and in person at local branches across England and Wales. Their housing pages cover tenancy rights, dealing with landlord disputes, and how to challenge unlawful eviction.

Housing Ombudsman Service
Organisation housing-ombudsman.org.uk ↗

The Housing Ombudsman resolves disputes between residents and their landlords, primarily in the social housing sector. It can investigate complaints about repairs, communications, and service failures and can order landlords to pay compensation or take specific action. Private tenants should generally use Shelter or Citizens Advice in the first instance.

First-tier Tribunal (Property Chamber)
Tribunal gov.uk ↗

The Property Chamber of the First-tier Tribunal hears applications relating to leasehold disputes, rent levels, licensing of houses in multiple occupation (HMOs), and appeals against local authority improvement notices. Tenants can apply to the Tribunal to challenge certain landlord decisions or to enforce housing standards in some circumstances.
