Legal Eviction Procedures

Evictions must follow strict legal procedures outlined in the Housing Act 1988 . Landlords cannot simply lock you out or remove your belongings without a court order.

Important: If you receive an eviction notice, seek legal advice immediately from Citizens Advice, Shelter, or a solicitor.

Types of Eviction Notices

Section 21 Notice (No-Fault Eviction)

  • Landlord does not need to give a reason
  • Minimum 2 months' notice required (as of June 2022)
  • Must be served correctly according to legal requirements
  • Can only be used for Assured Shorthold Tenancies (ASTs)
  • Cannot be used if there are rent arrears or breaches by landlord

Section 8 Notice (For-Cause Eviction)

  • Landlord must have a legal reason (e.g., rent arrears, breach of tenancy)
  • Minimum notice periods vary depending on the reason (usually 2-8 weeks)
  • Landlord must apply to court for a possession order
  • You have the right to defend yourself in court
  • Court may refuse eviction if circumstances are reasonable

Grounds for Section 8 Eviction

  • Rent arrears (usually 8 weeks or more)
  • Breach of tenancy terms (e.g., unauthorized occupants, damaging property)
  • Nuisance or annoyance to neighbors
  • Waste of the property
  • Conviction for criminality

Your Protections

  • ✓ You have the right to proper notice in writing
  • ✓ Evictions must go through the courts (except in exceptional circumstances)
  • ✓ You can challenge an eviction notice if it's not served correctly
  • ✓ You can defend yourself at court proceedings
  • ✓ Illegal eviction is a criminal offence
  • ✓ You cannot be evicted in certain circumstances (e.g., winter evictions in some cases)
  • ✓ Your landlord cannot evict for revenge or discrimination

If You Receive an Eviction Notice

Act immediately:

  1. Check the notice was served correctly
  2. Verify the notice period is correct
  3. Check for any breaches in the notice wording
  4. Seek legal advice immediately (within days)
  5. Contact Shelter or Citizens Advice urgently
  6. Gather evidence to defend yourself
  7. Attend court if a hearing is scheduled

Illegal Eviction

It is a criminal offence for a landlord to evict you without following proper legal procedures. Illegal eviction includes:

  • Locking you out without a court order
  • Removing your belongings
  • Cutting off utilities (water, gas, electricity)
  • Changing the locks
  • Harassing you to force you to leave
  • Threatening you with eviction

If this happens: Contact the police and seek immediate legal advice. You may be able to claim damages.

Winter Evictions

While not a complete ban, there are restrictions on evicting during winter months (typically December to March). Judges must consider:

  • Whether vulnerable people live in the property
  • The availability of accommodation
  • Reasonableness in the circumstances
