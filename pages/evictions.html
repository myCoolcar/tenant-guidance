📜 Housing Act 1988

Eviction rights.

Your landlord cannot simply ask you to leave. The law sets out a strict process they must follow — and you have real protections at every stage.

🚨

Facing eviction right now?

Do not leave your home until you have taken advice. Contact Shelter on 0808 800 4444 (free) or Citizens Advice immediately. An invalid notice means you do not have to go.

⚖️

The Legal Eviction Process

What must happen before you can lawfully be required to leave

For the vast majority of private tenants, your landlord must follow all of these steps in order. There are no shortcuts.

1

Serve a valid written notice. Either a Section 21 (no-fault) or Section 8 (grounds-based) notice under the Housing Act 1988. The notice must be on the correct form and contain all required information.

2

Wait for the notice period to expire. The minimum period depends on the type of notice. You are under no obligation to leave before it does.

3

Apply to court for a possession order. If you have not left, the landlord must apply to the County Court. You will be notified and have the right to attend the hearing and challenge the claim.

4

Obtain and enforce a warrant. Only after a court grants a possession order and, if necessary, a warrant of eviction can a court-appointed bailiff lawfully require you to leave.

Legal basis: Housing Act 1988 and the Protection from Eviction Act 1977.
📄

Types of Eviction Notice

There are two types of notice a landlord of an assured shorthold tenancy can use.

Section 21

No-fault notice

  • The landlord does not need to give a reason
  • Minimum two months' notice must be given
  • Must use the prescribed Form 6A
  • Cannot be used if the landlord has not provided a valid gas safety certificate, EPC, and the How to Rent guide
  • Cannot be served within the first four months of the original tenancy
  • May be invalid if served after the landlord failed to address a formal repair complaint (Deregulation Act 2015)
Section 8

Grounds-based notice

  • The landlord must state specific legal grounds from Schedule 2 of the Housing Act 1988
  • Common grounds include at least two months' rent arrears, damage to the property, or breach of tenancy terms
  • Notice periods vary by ground — typically 2 weeks to 2 months
  • You can defend yourself at the court hearing
  • The court has discretion to refuse possession on some grounds if it considers it reasonable to do so
📬

If You Receive an Eviction Notice

Do not ignore a notice and do not leave before taking advice. Many notices contain errors that make them invalid. You may not be required to leave at all.
1

Check the type of notice. Is it a Section 21 or Section 8? Is it on the correct prescribed form? Does it include the required information?

2

Check the dates. Has the correct notice period been given? A Section 21 notice requires a minimum of two months.

3

Check the landlord's compliance. For Section 21, did your landlord provide a gas safety certificate, EPC, and How to Rent guide at the start of the tenancy? Failure to do so can make the notice invalid.

4

Get advice immediately. Contact Shelter or Citizens Advice within days — not weeks. The sooner you act, the more options you have.

5

Attend any court hearing. If the matter proceeds to court, attend. You have the right to present your case, and a judge cannot grant possession without hearing you.

Illegal Eviction

A criminal offence under the Protection from Eviction Act 1977

The following actions are criminal offences, regardless of any rent arrears or dispute. If any of these happen to you, call the police and seek legal advice immediately.

✗

Changing the locks while you are a lawful occupier

✗

Removing or disposing of your belongings

✗

Cutting off gas, electricity, or water to force you out

✗

Threatening or intimidating you to make you leave

✗

Entering the property repeatedly without notice to harass you

If you have been illegally evicted, you may be entitled to re-enter the property and claim damages. Courts have awarded substantial sums in illegal eviction cases. Contact Shelter immediately.
