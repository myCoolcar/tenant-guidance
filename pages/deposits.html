🔒 Housing Act 2004

Tenancy deposits.

Your deposit must be protected by law. Here is what your landlord must do, what they can deduct, and what to do if they don't play by the rules.

📌

The Key Rules at a Glance

30
days your landlord has to protect your deposit after receiving it
5 weeks
maximum deposit for annual rent under £50,000
1–3×
penalty your landlord can face if they fail to protect it

Under the Housing Act 2004, your landlord or letting agent must register your deposit with one of three government-approved schemes within 30 days of receiving it. They must also give you written information — called "prescribed information" — about which scheme holds it and how to get your money back.

Legal basis: Housing Act 2004, ss.212–215. Failure to protect the deposit or serve prescribed information entitles the tenant to apply to the County Court for a penalty of between one and three times the deposit amount.
🏛️

The Three Approved Schemes

Your landlord must use one of these — no others are permitted

Each scheme offers a free dispute resolution service. If you and your landlord disagree over deductions, you can refer the matter to the scheme for a binding independent decision — without going to court.

Deposit Protection Service (DPS)

A custodial scheme — the DPS holds the deposit on your behalf throughout the tenancy. Free to use for landlords and tenants.

depositprotection.com ↗

MyDeposits

An insurance-backed scheme — the landlord keeps the deposit but pays to insure it. Free dispute resolution available to tenants.

mydeposits.co.uk ↗

Tenancy Deposit Scheme (TDS)

Offers both custodial and insurance-backed options. Their adjudication service can resolve disputes over deductions at no cost to you.

tenancydepositscheme.com ↗

What Can and Cannot Be Deducted

Normal wear and tear is never deductible. Your landlord cannot charge for items that have simply aged or worn through ordinary everyday use — only for damage you caused beyond that.

Allowed deductions

  • Unpaid rent
  • Damage you caused beyond normal wear and tear
  • Replacement of items you lost (e.g. keys)
  • Professional cleaning — only if the property was dirtier than when you moved in

Not allowed

  • Worn carpets or flooring from normal use
  • Faded paint or wallpaper
  • Scuffs or minor marks from everyday living
  • Replacement of items that had simply reached the end of their useful life
⚖️

Disputing Deductions

If your landlord proposes deductions you disagree with, do not simply accept them. Every approved scheme provides a free, independent dispute resolution service — you do not need a solicitor or a court to use it.

1

Respond in writing. Tell your landlord which deductions you dispute and why. Keep a copy.

2

Gather your evidence. Collect your check-in photos, check-out photos, the inventory, and any relevant emails.

3

Raise a dispute with the scheme. Contact the scheme that holds your deposit and request their dispute resolution service. You normally have a limited window to do this after the tenancy ends, so act quickly.

4

Submit your evidence. The scheme's adjudicator will review what both sides provide and make a binding decision. The process is free and usually completed within 28 days.

🚨

If Your Deposit Was Not Protected

This is a serious breach of law. Your landlord had a legal obligation to protect your deposit. You may be entitled to a significant penalty.

If your landlord failed to protect the deposit, or protected it but never gave you the prescribed information, you can apply to the County Court for a penalty of between one and three times the deposit amount, in addition to the return of the deposit itself.

Contact Shelter (0808 800 4444) or Citizens Advice for guidance before starting a claim. Use the Letter Generator to send a formal notice to your landlord first.

Move-Out Checklist

Doing these things when you leave protects your deposit and gives you strong evidence if a dispute arises.

1

Take a full set of dated photographs of every room, including walls, floors, appliances, and fittings — even things that look fine.

2

Request a check-out inspection and, if possible, be present for it so you can address any concerns on the spot.

3

Ask for written confirmation of the scheme your deposit is held in, if you don't already have it.

4

If any deductions are proposed, ask for a full written breakdown with evidence for each item before agreeing to anything.

5

If you disagree with deductions, raise a dispute through the scheme's adjudication service promptly — do not delay.
