Key Housing Legislation

These are the principal Acts of Parliament that protect tenants in England and Wales.

1985
Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 Section 11 obliges landlords to keep in repair the structure, exterior, and key installations (heating, water, gas, electricity, sanitation) of any residential property let for less than seven years.
View ↗
1988
Housing Act 1988 Established the modern private rental framework in England, creating assured and assured shorthold tenancies (ASTs) and setting out the legal basis for possession, rent, and tenancy agreements.
View ↗
2004
Housing Act 2004 Introduced mandatory tenancy deposit protection in government-approved schemes and the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS), giving councils powers to enforce improvements to hazardous properties.
View ↗
2019
Tenant Fees Act 2019 Bans landlords and letting agents from charging fees not explicitly permitted by the Act (such as admin, referencing, or renewal fees). Unlawful fees are recoverable and enforceable by Trading Standards.
View ↗
2020
Electrical Safety Standards in the Private Rented Sector (England) Regulations 2020 Requires landlords to have electrical installations inspected and tested by a qualified person at least every five years, and to provide tenants with a copy of the report.

Your Fundamental Rights as a Tenant

As a private tenant in England and Wales, the law provides you with these core protections.

✦
Right to a safe, habitable home Your property must be fit for human habitation — with working heating, plumbing, and electrical systems. The Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018 reinforces this.
✦
Protection from unfair eviction Your landlord must follow strict legal procedures. Changing locks, removing belongings, or cutting off utilities without a court order is a criminal offence under the Protection from Eviction Act 1977.
✦
Deposit protection Your deposit must be held in a government-approved scheme within 30 days of payment, and you must be given prescribed information about it. Failure attracts a penalty of 1–3× the deposit amount.
✦
Right to quiet enjoyment You have the right to use the property peacefully without unreasonable interference from your landlord. Entry normally requires at least 24 hours' written notice.
✦
Protection from discrimination Under the Equality Act 2010, you cannot be refused housing or treated less favourably because of a protected characteristic such as race, sex, disability, or religion.
✦
Protection from harassment Under Section 1 of the Protection from Eviction Act 1977, it is a criminal offence for a landlord to harass a tenant with the intent of causing them to leave.
✦
Right to challenge unlawful fees Any fee charged outside the Tenant Fees Act 2019's permitted payments is unlawful and can be reclaimed. You can also report the landlord or agent to Trading Standards.
✦
Unfair terms are unenforceable Terms in your tenancy agreement that seek to remove statutory rights or impose unfair burdens on you are unenforceable under the Consumer Rights Act 2015.

Types of Tenancy

The type of tenancy you hold affects your rights and the procedures your landlord must follow.

Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST)

The most common form of private rental agreement, typically fixed for 6 or 12 months and then becoming periodic. Subject to the Housing Act 1988. Landlords may end it via Section 21 (no-fault) or Section 8 (grounds-based) notice.

Assured Tenancy

Less common in the private sector today. Provides stronger protections — landlords can only end an assured tenancy by proving one of the grounds listed in Schedule 2 of the Housing Act 1988. No Section 21 route is available.

Regulated (Protected) Tenancy

Applies to tenancies created before 15 January 1989. Governed by the Rent Act 1977. Provides the strongest tenant protections, including fair rent registration and security of tenure that can pass to a spouse or family member.

What You Should Do

Practical steps that protect your position if a dispute arises.

1

Always get your tenancy agreement in writing and keep a copy in a safe place.

2

Take dated photographs of the property at the start of the tenancy to record its condition.

3

Report all repairs in writing — by email where possible — so you have a timestamped record.

4

Keep copies of all correspondence with your landlord or letting agent, including text messages.

5

Check that your deposit has been protected and that you have received the prescribed information within 30 days of payment.

6

If you receive any notice from your landlord, do not vacate until you have checked its validity — invalid notices do not require you to leave.
