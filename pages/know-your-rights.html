Know your rights.

You don't need to understand the law to use this page. We explain what it means for you — and what you can do.

💬

As a renter in England, the law is on your side in many situations. This page covers your most important rights in plain English — from repairs and deposits to eviction and privacy. Each section explains what the law says, what it means for you, and the steps you can take if something goes wrong.

🔧

Repairs and living conditions

Your home must be safe and properly maintained

Your landlord is responsible for keeping your home in good repair. This includes heating, hot water, the roof, walls, plumbing, and electrics. If something breaks, it is their job to fix it — and they must do so within a reasonable time after you tell them.

What this means for you

  • Your landlord must fix broken heating or hot water
  • Leaking roofs, damp, and structural problems are their responsibility
  • Faulty wiring or plumbing must be repaired
  • These obligations apply even if your tenancy agreement says otherwise — such clauses are unenforceable
  • Your home must be fit for human habitation from day one

What you can do

  • Read the full repairs guide for step-by-step guidance
  • Report the repair in writing — email is best
  • Generate a formal repair request letter
  • Log the issue in your evidence timeline
  • If ignored, contact your council's Environmental Health team
Legal basis Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, s.11  ·  Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018

Eviction and security of tenure

Your landlord cannot simply tell you to leave

Your landlord cannot force you to leave without following a strict legal process. They must give you the correct written notice and, in most cases, obtain a court order before you are required to go. Changing your locks, removing your belongings, or cutting off your utilities to force you out is a criminal offence.

What this means for you

  • You do not have to leave just because a landlord tells you to
  • A valid notice must be in the correct format and give the right amount of time
  • Even after a valid notice, the landlord must go to court to make you leave
  • Illegal eviction — such as changing locks — is a criminal offence
  • You should not move out until you have checked the notice is valid

What you can do

  • Read the full evictions guide
  • Do not leave the property voluntarily until you have taken advice
  • If locks are changed or utilities cut, call Shelter on 0808 800 4444 immediately
  • Generate a response letter to challenge an invalid notice
  • Contact your council's Tenancy Relations Officer for free help
Legal basis Housing Act 1988  ·  Protection from Eviction Act 1977

Your deposit

Your landlord must protect it — and return it fairly

Your deposit must be placed in a government-approved scheme within 30 days of you paying it. You must also receive written information telling you which scheme it is in and how to get it back. If your landlord does not do this, you could be entitled to up to three times the deposit amount as compensation.

What this means for you

  • Your deposit is protected by law — your landlord cannot simply keep it
  • Deductions must be justified — normal wear and tear cannot be charged
  • You have the right to dispute deductions through a free adjudication service
  • If your deposit was not protected, you can claim a penalty in court

What you can do

  • Read the full deposit protection guide
  • Check your deposit is protected at gov.uk/tenancy-deposit-protection
  • Generate a letter requesting your deposit back
  • Use the scheme's free dispute service if deductions are unfair
Legal basis Housing Act 2004, ss.212–215

Privacy and peaceful enjoyment

Your home is yours — your landlord cannot come and go freely

You have the right to live in your home without unreasonable interference from your landlord. In most situations, your landlord must give you at least 24 hours' written notice before entering — and they can only enter at a reasonable time. Repeatedly turning up unannounced may be illegal harassment.

What this means for you

  • Your landlord needs your permission or must give proper notice to enter
  • Turning up unannounced repeatedly is not acceptable
  • Entering without notice (except in a genuine emergency) may be unlawful
  • Harassment intended to make you leave your home is a criminal offence

What you can do

  • Keep a written log of every visit — dates, times, and what was said
  • Record entries in your evidence timeline
  • Send a formal letter reminding your landlord of the notice requirement
  • If harassment continues, report it to your local council or the police
Legal basis Common law right to quiet enjoyment  ·  Protection from Eviction Act 1977, s.1

Fees and charges

Most letting fees are now banned by law

Since 2019, most fees charged by landlords or letting agents are illegal. You should only be asked to pay rent, a deposit (capped at 5 weeks' rent), a holding deposit (capped at 1 week's rent), and a small number of specific charges. Anything else — admin fees, referencing fees, check-in fees — is banned.

What this means for you

  • You cannot be charged for referencing, credit checks, or admin
  • Renewal fees are banned — you should not pay to extend your tenancy
  • Check-in and check-out fees are also illegal
  • Unlawful fees can be reclaimed — and reported to Trading Standards

What you can do

  • Check whether a fee is legal on the Fees page
  • Ask your landlord or agent to refund any unlawful charges in writing
  • Report illegal fees to Trading Standards
  • Generate a letter requesting a refund
Legal basis Tenant Fees Act 2019

Discrimination and unfair treatment

You cannot be treated less favourably because of who you are

It is illegal to refuse someone housing, or treat a tenant differently, because of protected characteristics such as their race, sex, disability, religion, or family status. Terms in your tenancy agreement that try to take away your legal rights are also unenforceable.

What this means for you

  • A landlord cannot refuse to rent to you based on your background or identity
  • You cannot be evicted or harassed because of a protected characteristic
  • Clauses in your tenancy that contradict the law do not apply to you
  • You have the right to request reasonable adjustments if you have a disability

What you can do

  • Keep a record of any discriminatory comments or decisions
  • Contact the Equality and Human Rights Commission for guidance
  • Seek legal advice from Citizens Advice or Shelter
  • Make a complaint to your local council or the Housing Ombudsman
Legal basis Equality Act 2010  ·  Consumer Rights Act 2015

What type of tenancy do you have?

Your rights depend partly on your tenancy type

Most private renters in England have an Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST). Your tenancy agreement should tell you which type you have. If you're not sure, the type started before 1989 is different — and you may have stronger protections.
Most common

Assured Shorthold Tenancy (AST)

This is what most private renters have today. Usually fixed for 6 or 12 months, then it continues month-to-month. Your landlord can end it using a Section 21 (no-fault) or Section 8 (grounds-based) notice — but must follow the correct legal process to do so.

Less common

Assured Tenancy

Provides stronger protection — your landlord can only end this tenancy by proving specific legal grounds. There is no Section 21 no-fault route. Less common in the private sector today, but some older tenancies still fall into this category.

Pre-1989 tenancies

Regulated (Protected) Tenancy

If your tenancy started before 15 January 1989, you may have a regulated tenancy under the Rent Act 1977. These carry the strongest protections, including the right to a fair registered rent and strong security of tenure. Get specialist advice if this applies to you.

Protect yourself from day one

Six things every tenant should do

1

Get your tenancy agreement in writing and keep a copy somewhere safe. If something goes wrong, this is your starting point.

2

Photograph the property on move-in day. Date your photos. This protects you from unfair deposit deductions when you leave.

3

Always report problems in writing — by email if possible. This creates a timestamped record that you notified your landlord.

4

Check your deposit is protected within 30 days of paying it. You should receive written confirmation of which scheme it is in.

5

Keep all correspondence. Texts, emails, and letters from your landlord are all potentially valuable evidence.

6

If you receive any notice to leave, do not move out immediately. Many notices are invalid. Check it carefully before taking any action.
